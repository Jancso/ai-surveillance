Group Detection during a Pandemic

Faced with the global COVID-19 pandemic now, the majority of people are taking all kinds of measures to protect themselves and others such as washing hands and practicing social distancing. Unfortunately, some people are reluctant to abide by these practices, meeting in large groups. Their irresponsible behavior contributes to the further spread of the virus, putting vulnerable people at risk, such as elderly people or people in poor health. In many countries, the police are patrolling places that are well known to be crowded such as beaches, parks and lakeside promenades, urging citizens to stay at home.

Since the police cannot control all places at all times and increasing police presence incurs additional costs, our aim was to find an automatic way of detecting people who do not respect social distancing. We decided to build a tool that recognizes groups of people in a video, either pre-recorded or streamed from a surveillance camera. The main use case would be private persons, companies and/or authorities setting up surveillance cameras and using the tool to detect gatherings of people.

Naturally, this raises concerns around data privacy. According to the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), the term ‘personal data’ is the entryway to the application of the regulation. The GDPR only applies if processing of data concerns personal data, with personal data being any information which is related to an identified or identifiable natural person. In our case, the question would be whether we can regard the existence of persons in a video as personal data, especially in public areas. While our tool assigns IDs to each person, thereby tracking the person, it does not try to identify the person in any way.

Our tool consists of a group-detection algorithm and a web-based interface using that algorithm, allowing users to analyze their videos for potential congregations of people. The group-detection algorithm accepts video files or streams as an input and works as follows: Since a video is a sequence of frames (images), it tries to identify people in each frame using machine learning. If an object is identified as a person, a unique ID is assigned to it and the person is tracked. To detect groups, an ellipse with predefined length is drawn around the center of a person, which represents the allowed social distance. If the ellipses of two people intersect, it means they are too close and form a group. As an output, the algorithm produces a JSON file which contains information about detected groups in the video: a unique identifier for each group, the timestamp of group formation, the size of the group, the identifiers of the persons in the group and how many frames the group existed for.
